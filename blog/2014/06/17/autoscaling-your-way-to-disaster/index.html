Autoscaling Your Way to Disaster

Jun 17th, 2014 | Comments

In the second follow-up to a previous blog post, I wanted to go into the mythical beast that is autoscaling.

Caveat

I’m going to repeat a few things that I originally said in this blog post for the sake of completeness.

Why autoscale?

The dream of the autoscale. A magical place where you only only allocate capacity when it’s needed and shed it when it’s not. It’s a cornerstone of marketing literature for almost every “cloud” related product out there. Deploy your apps here and just type one command or push this button to add capacity! In fact, just tell us and we’ll even do it for you!

It sounds awesome and assuming you have the right architecture, it might actually work. I, myself, was a proponent of autoscaling at one point (and still am to some degree) especially when I was running on top of AWS. I would look at that monthly bill and think “How can I get this down? Do we really need n servers to handle this workload all the time?

I went down all the paths I could. I even attempted to write my own service discovery tool to facilitate it. I wanted it all. Wire it up to my monitoring system and when the load got too high, spin me up some foo nodes.

I’ve since backed off that idea for several reasons.

Can autoscaling work?

Yes. Flat out autoscaling CAN work. The numbers that Adrian Cockcroft has thrown out from his days at Netflix are just one of many examples. Look at any high-visibility AWS customer and all of them tell a similar story about how they used some AWS feature to autoscale to meet some crushing demand and then shrunk back down after it was over. As I said before what’s missing in that discussion is what tells the truth.
